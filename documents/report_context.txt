1 Introduction
Monitoring water quality in aquacultural and riverine systems relies heavily on
predicting Electrical Conductivity (EC), a primary proxy for salinity intrusion
exacerbated by climate change [13,19,8,4]. As hydrological patterns become
more erratic, high-frequency EC data is increasingly non-stationary and domi-
nated by sudden, high-magnitude fluctuations, or "spikes" [5]. Accurately fore-
casting these extreme events is critical for early-warning systems but remains
remarkably difficult for conventional time-series models.

Recently, Long-term Time-series Forecasting (LTSF) has seen rapid advance-
ments,rangingfromcomplexTransformerarchitectures[15,18]tosimpler,highly
stable linear mappings [16,7]. While these deep learning models excel at cap-
turing general seasonal trends, they share a critical blind spot when evaluated
on highly volatile environmental data: they tend to "smooth out" the spikes.
This occurs because most models are optimized using symmetric loss functions,
typicallyMeanSquaredError(MSE),whichpenalizeoverestimationsandunder-
estimations equally. To minimize the global error, the network converges toward
the conditional mean, systematically underestimating hazardous peaks and ren-
dering the forecast practically useless during an actual crisis.

To address this critical underestimation, forecasting models must separate
noise from the underlying trend and heavily penalize under-predictions. In this
paper, we propose a Hybrid Decomposition-Ensemble model explicitly designed
to balance trend stability with peak sensitivity. By applying CEEMDAN directly
to the raw signal, the decoupled components are routed into a multi-branch net-
work: a deep Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) tackles high-frequency stochastic
spikes, while a linear branch processes low-frequency deterministic trends. Cru-
cially, we replace MSE with an Asymmetric Spike-Aware Loss that imposes a
heavy penalty whenever the network misses a high-magnitude peak.
The primary contributions of this study are summarized as follows:
1. Frequenc-Decoupled Architecture: We propose a specialized multi-branch
network that models stochastic spikes and deterministic trends indepen-
dently,significantlyreducingtheinterferencebetweenhigh-frequencyvolatil-

2. Asymmetric Optimization Strategy: We introduce a custom loss function
combining focal weighting and directed asymmetric penalties. This explic-

2 Related Work
2.1 Deep Learning and Hybrid Forecasting in Hydrology
Water quality forecasting has shifted from traditional statistical methods (e.g.,
ARIMA, SVR) to deep learning to better capture the non-linear, volatile nature
of hydrological data, with RNNs (LSTM, GRU) becoming the standard.
To enhance predictive accuracy, researchers often pair these networks with
signal decomposition techniques. For example, coupling CEEMDAN [14] with
LSTM [13,10] significantly reduces background noise and lowers the root mean
square error (RMSE) for indicators like ammonia nitrogen. Similarly, LTSF-
Linear combined with CEEMDAN has been utilized to manage seasonal vari-
ations in water treatment [12]. However, these noise-filtering hybrid pipelines
predominantly rely on symmetric loss functions (e.g., MSE) during training.
This inadvertently biases models toward the mean, leading to a systematic un-
derestimation of extreme contamination events.
2.2 The LTSF Architecture Debate
In the broader context of Long-term Time-series Forecasting (LTSF), the dom-
inance of Transformers has been recently challenged. Zeng et al. [16] demon-
strated that for temporal data with limited semantic density, simple linear map-
pings(likeDLinear)oftenoutperformcomplexattentionmechanismsbyavoiding
temporal noise overfitting. Building on this, PatchTST [11] showed that dividing
time-series into patches captures local semantic patterns more effectively than
point-wise processing.
Our work bridges these findings through a hybrid strategy. We leverage the
proven stability of linear mappings for low-frequency deterministic trends, while
reservingthenon-linearcapacityofDeepMulti-LayerPerceptrons(MLPs)—which
have recently shown a resurgence in time-series forecasting [2]—strictly for high-
frequency stochastic spikes.

3.1 Adaptive Signal Decomposition
Raw environmental time-series data is typically a superimposition of long-term
seasonal trends and abrupt, high-frequency stochastic fluctuations (e.g., sud-
den salinity intrusions). Feeding such raw sequences directly into standard deep
learning models often results in gradient noise and poor convergence.To mit-
igate this, we employ the Complete Ensemble Empirical Mode Decomposition
with Adaptive Noise (CEEMDAN) algorithm to decouple the original time series
X(t)intoKIntrinsic Mode Functions (IMFs) and a residual trendR(t). The
decomposition process is mathematically expressed as:

A critical distinction of our approach is that we apply CEEMDAN directly to
the unscaled, raw signal. Conventional pipelines typically normalize the entire
dataset before decomposition, which inadvertently compresses the magnitude of
extreme EC peaks. By retaining the original scale, we preserve the morphological
fidelity of the critical spikes.
SettingK= 12, we partition the resulting components into two distinct
frequency-based sets:
1. High-Frequency Set (X high): ComprisingIMF 1throughIMF 3, this set iso-
lates rapid fluctuations and stochastic noise.
2. Low-Frequency Set (X low): ComprisingIMF 4throughIMF 11alongside the
residueR(t), this set represents the underlying deterministic trends.

3.2 Hybrid Network Architecture
Instead of employing a generalized recurrent or attention-based network, we
route the decoupled frequency sets into specialized neural branches. This ar-
chitecture significantly reduces signal interference between short-term volatility
and long-term periodicity.
1. Deep MLP Branch: Because EC spikes exhibit high non-linearity, we utilize
a Deep Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) featuring wide hidden layers (512
units) and ReLU activations to act as a dedicated spike detector for the
high-frequency components.
2. Trend Linear Branch: Inspired by the robust performance of DLinear mod-
els, the low-frequency components are processed by a single linear mapping
layer. This design choice prevents overfitting to minor temporal fluctuations,
ensuring stable extrapolation of macroscopic trends.
3. Auxiliary MLP: A compact MLP (154 units) is employed to encode the
supplementary statistical features.
The latent representations from the three branches are concatenated and passed
through a Fusion Layer. Crucially, the network predicts a temporal delta (∆ˆy)
rather than absolute values. The final forecast is reconstructed by adding this
delta to the last observed valuex t:
ˆyt+H=xt+Fusion(y high, ylow, yfeat)(2)

4 Experimental Setup
4.1 Dataset Description and Partitioning
To evaluate the framework, we utilize an hourly water quality dataset (43,813
observations) from the USGS sensor network, spanning January 2021 to Decem-
ber 2025. The primary target variable is Electrical Conductivity (EC), a critical
proxy for salinity intrusion. To prevent data leakage inherent in temporal de-
pendencies, we employ a strict chronological split: 60% for training (2021–2023),
20% for validation (2024), and 20% for testing (2025).

4.2 Baseline Models
To rigorously assess the superiority of the proposed framework, we benchmark
it against four state-of-the-art forecasting models representing different architec-
tural paradigms:
1. Transformer:Thestandardself-attentionmechanism,representingdeepglobal
semantic modeling.
2. PatchTST: A state-of-the-art Transformer variant that utilizes patching and
channel independence to capture local semantics.
3. CEEMDAN-LTSF-Linear: A highly efficient linear mapping model (specif-
ically DLinear) that excels at extracting global trends without overfitting
temporal noise.
4. CEEMDAN-LSTM: A classic hybrid approach combining signal decomposi-
tion with recurrent networks, optimized via standard symmetric loss.
4.3 Implementation Details
All models were implemented in PyTorch on an NVIDIA RTX 4060 GPU. The
historical look-back window (L) is fixed at 168 hours to capture weekly season-
ality, forecasting future horizonsT∈ {6,12,24,48,96,168}hours. Our proposed
model uses CEEMDAN configured withK= 12modes and 20 ensemble tri-
als. Training utilizes the Adam optimizer (initial learning rate10−3, batch size
64) with a 10-epoch early stopping patience. The Asymmetric Spike-Aware Loss
parameters are empirically set toα= 5.0andγ= 2.0. Baselines retain their
default optimal configurations.
4.4 Evaluation Metrics
To quantify forecasting performance, we rely on three established accuracy mea-
sures [6]: Mean Absolute Error (MAE), Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE), and
the Coefficient of Determination (R2).

5.2 The Cost of False Negatives in Salinity Monitoring
In real-world environmental monitoring, evaluating a model solely on global
RMSE is misleading. If a system predicts 160µS/cm when the actual value
is 150µS/cm, the error penalty is the same as predicting 390µS/cm when
a severe intrusion hits 400µS/cm. Operationally, the first scenario is harmless.
Thesecondscenario—aFalseNegativeduringacriticalevent—canleadtosevere
agricultural damage.
We explicitly designed the Asymmetric Spike-Aware Loss (L SA) to break the
mean-seekinghabitofstandardnetworks.Byenforcingaheavypenalty(α= 5.0)
on underestimations, our model intentionally sacrifices precision during low-risk,
flat periods. The network accepts higher variance during normal conditions to
ensure it has the mathematical "freedom" to scale up aggressively when the
high-frequency IMFs indicate an impending shock

6 Conclusion
This paper presented a novel hybrid framework to overcome the oversmoothing
limitations of standard LTSF models in forecasting high-frequency salinity intru-
sions. Relying on CEEMDAN decomposition, a multi-branch architecture, and
an Asymmetric Spike-Aware Loss (L SA), our method shifts the paradigm from
aggregate error minimization to extreme event detection. Evaluations demon-
strate the model’s unique capability to maintain morphological fidelity dur-
ing extreme surges (>250µS/cm), effectively eliminating the ceiling effect of
baselines. SHAP analysis further confirms that the network successfully priori-
tizes high-frequency stochastic noise during critical events.Despite these advan-
tages, the framework introduces certain trade-offs. The strict penalty on under-
predictions naturally increases variance during stable periods, which could occa-
sionally trigger false positive alerts. Additionally, applying CEEMDAN directly
torawsignalsentailshighercomputationaloverhead.Futureresearchwillexplore
lightweight signal decomposition techniques and automated hyperparameter op-
timization (e.g., forαandγ) to enhance the model’s efficiency and adaptability
across diverse datasets. Ultimately, this research provides an interpretable and
operationally viable early-warning mechanism for estuarine agriculture.
